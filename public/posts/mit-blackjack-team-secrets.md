Bill Kaplan wasn't just a gambler—he was a businessman who understood that **blackjack could be treated like an investment**. Before joining forces with Massar, Kaplan had already proven the concept by:

## The Strategy: How Team Play Works

The MIT Blackjack Team didn't invent card counting—they perfected the **team play strategy** that amplified its effectiveness exponentially.

### The Three Roles

#### 1. **Spotters** (Low-Stakes Players)

- Made minimum bets ($10-$25) at tables
- Counted cards using the Hi-Lo system
- **Never varied their bet size** (to avoid detection)
- Signaled "Big Players" when the count went positive

**Key Advantage:** Spotters looked like casual gamblers losing money slowly. Casinos ignored them.

#### 2. **Big Players** (High Rollers)

- Dressed like wealthy tourists or businessmen
- Only sat down when signaled by a spotter
- Made massive bets ($100-$10,000) when the count was favorable
- Left quickly when the count dropped

**Key Advantage:** Big Players appeared to be lucky rich amateurs, not skilled card counters.

#### 3. **The Controller** (Bankroll Manager)

- Managed the team's money supply
- Tracked wins and losses meticulously
- Calculated each player's earnings based on their contribution
- Made strategic decisions about which casinos to target

### The Card Counting System

The team primarily used the **Hi-Lo card counting system**:

| Card Value | Count |
|---|---|
| 2-6 | +1 |
| 7-9 | 0 |
| 10-Ace | -1 |

**True Count Calculation:**
```
True Count = Running Count ÷ Decks Remaining
```

**Betting Strategy:**
- True Count +1 or less: Minimum bet (spotters only)
- True Count +2: Call in Big Player
- True Count +3: Bet $500
- True Count +4: Bet $1,000
- True Count +5 or higher: Bet $2,000-$10,000

### The Secret Signals

Team members communicated using subtle hand signals and code words:

- **"Counting the deck"** - Positive count building
- **Touching their chips in specific ways** - Indicating exact count
- **Leaving drinks or chips positioned strategically** - Count information
- **Specific words or phrases** - Time to join or leave

These signals were designed to look completely natural to casino surveillance.

- **The Techie:** Pretended to have sold a startup, casually mentioned Silicon Valley
- **The Trust Fund Kid:** Acted young, reckless, and rich
- **The Foreign Businessman:** Spoke with accents, pretended to be on business trips

### Acting Drunk (While Stone Sober)

Big Players would:
- Order expensive drinks but barely sip them
- Act slightly tipsy and overly friendly
- Make "stupid" plays occasionally to look amateur
- Tip dealers generously to build goodwill
- Celebrate wins loudly and complain about losses

**The goal:** Look like a lucky rich amateur, not a skilled professional.

## The Casino Countermeasures

As the team's winnings grew, casinos fought back:

### Surveillance Tactics

- **Facial recognition software** - Shared databases of known card counters
- **Shuffle tracking** - Monitoring when Big Players entered games
- **Bet pattern analysis** - Flagging players who only bet big on favorable counts
- **Team identification** - Looking for spotters signaling Big Players

### What Casinos Did When They Caught You

1. **Warning:** Asked you to play another game
2. **Flat Betting:** Required you to bet the same amount every hand
3. **Barring:** Banned from the casino permanently
4. **Griffin Book:** Added to industry-wide blacklist
5. **Backroom:** In rare cases, aggressive interrogation (borderline illegal)

## Why the Team Eventually Disbanded

### The Main Reasons

1. **Increased Casino Surveillance**
   - Facial recognition became too sophisticated
   - Major casinos shared information about known counters
   - Too many members were burned (identified and banned)

2. **Internal Conflicts**
   - Disagreements over profit splitting
   - Some members wanted to bet bigger, others more conservative
   - Trust issues when millions were involved

3. **Better Opportunities**
   - Many members went into finance, tech, and entrepreneurship
   - Legitimate careers offered better long-term returns
   - Risk vs. reward shifted as casino heat increased

4. **The Money Got Too Big**
   - Managing $1M+ bankrolls created logistical challenges
   - IRS scrutiny increased
   - Some members wanted to cash out their profits
